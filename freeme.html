The Problem Space

The Problem Space

Scientific literature reveals a significant "leisure gap" for informal caregivers. While existing apps focus on social support or patient care, none address the caregiver's personal need for restorative time. Caregivers report high stress and guilt when attempting to prioritize their own well-being.

User Pain: Caregivers feel they lack time and struggle with the cognitive load of deciding what to do during rare free moments.

The Goal: Design a mobile solution to help caregivers manage and optimize their leisure time through low-friction activity suggestions.

"Since I have to make decisions everyday and do things for me and others, I like having someone that gives me an idea and that doesn't make me think about what to do."

Research & Synthesis

Research & Synthesis

I conducted four semi-structured interviews with primary caregivers to move beyond literature and understand real-world habits. Using Grounded Theory, I distilled transcriptions into five macro-areas: Caregiving context, Time management, Leisure activities, App design expectations, and Social sharing.

Archetypes Identified:

Walter (50)

Struggling with unpredictable schedules and "doom-scrolling" during breaks.

Irene (55)

High guilt; needs short, interruptible activities.

Bianca (62)

Evening exhaustion; needs help shifting from "care mode" to "relaxation mode".

Strategic UX Rationale

Strategic UX Rationale

The interaction strategy focused on Cognitive Load Reduction and Behavioral Engagement.

"Be Inspired" (Lasciati Ispirare)

A core feature designed to solve "decision fatigue". Instead of browsing, users input their available time and mood to receive an immediate activity suggestion.

Gamification (Octalysis Framework)

Integrated progress bars, badges, and "Course Projects" to drive self-realization and creative feedback.

Sound Symbolism

The name "FreeMe" utilizes the [i] phone to psychologically evoke a sense of lightness and friendliness.

Visual System

Used desaturated greens and blues (#405D60) with rounded typography (Nunito & Manrope) to communicate calm and accessibility.

The Iteration Engine (Data-Driven Pivots)

The Iteration Engine (Data-Driven Pivots)

I conducted Think-Aloud Usability Testing with four users across 11 specific tasks.

Baseline Performance: 66% Success | 25% Partial Success | 9% Failure.

Cycle 3 Pivot — Task 1

Problem Identified (Task Success Failure): Users failed to save courses (Task 1).

UI Solution (Cycle 3 Pivot): Added explicit "Salva" (Save) text labels to icons.

Rationale: Recognition over recall; icons alone lacked universal meaning.

Cycle 3 Pivot — Task 11

Problem Identified (Task Success Failure): 0% success on switching between activities and courses (Task 11).

UI Solution (Cycle 3 Pivot): Repositioned the toggle to the bottom screen, increased size, and updated logic.

Rationale: Users naturally focused on the lower content area; the top-mounted toggle was outside focus.

[PLACEHOLDER: BEFORE - Top Placed Toggle]
[PLACEHOLDER: AFTER - Bottom Anchored Toggle]

Cycle 3 Pivot — Task 9

Problem Identified (Task Success Failure): Confusion between "Started Courses" and "Resume" pages (Task 9).

UI Solution (Cycle 3 Pivot): Merged similar pages into a unified "In corso" (In Progress) hub.

Rationale: Reduced complexity and eliminated redundant navigation paths.

Final QA & Ethical Constraints

Final QA & Ethical Constraints

A final Nielsen Heuristic Evaluation verified the Cycle 3 fixes.

System Status

Every change is accompanied by feedback.

Error Prevention

Added confirmation dialogs for time-sensitive tasks like the timer.

Ethical Pivot

We explicitly removed "Emotional Profiling" from the "Be Inspired" feature. While technically feasible, collecting sensitive emotional data from a high-stress user base was deemed an unnecessary risk.
