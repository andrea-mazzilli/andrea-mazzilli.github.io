The Problem Space

Western societies are aging. Informal caregivers — family members who provide unpaid assistance to an elderly or ill relative — bear enormous cognitive and emotional loads. And when they finally have free time, they often can't use it well.

A scoping review by Bidenko & Bohnet-Joschko (2022) confirmed the gap: existing apps focus on social connection between caregivers, not on personal leisure. No app addressed how to spend free time.

Further research (Lim et al., 2021; Bedini et al., 2018) added a key finding: the quality of leisure experience, not just the quantity of free time, predicts reduced stress and better self-reported health. Participation alone is not enough — satisfaction matters.

Design challenge: Build a mobile app that helps informal caregivers find, start, and actually enjoy leisure activities — reducing the cognitive load of deciding what to do.

Cycle 1

Needfinding

Literature Review

Using NotebookLM to query and cross-reference three peer-reviewed papers, we identified two core user problems:

  • Caregivers feel guilt when dedicating time to themselves
  • They lack a structured way to discover activities that fit their available time and energy

Semi-Structured Interviews

We conducted 4 interviews with real informal caregivers, selecting participants who provided care regularly and for significant portions of their time. The interview was structured across 6 sections: caregiving context → leisure habits → critical pain-points → desires → digital tool usage → reaction to app concept. Audio was transcribed using Rev and a Word AI transcription tool.

Grounded Theory Analysis

Interview transcripts were coded using Grounded Theory to extract keywords → codes → macro-themes. 5 macro-themes identified:

  1. Caregiving (context and routines)
  2. Leisure time and management
  3. Activities during leisure time
  4. Sharing leisure time
  5. App design expectations

This analysis directly shaped the three activity macro-categories in the final app: relaxation, physical activity, and video courses / hobbies.

Design Alternatives

Competitor Analysis & Feature Mapping

Competitors were identified across three areas: Wellness / mindfulness (Headspace, Calm, Breethe) and Creative learning (Skillshare, Domestika, MasterClass). ChatGPT was used to generate Excel-based feature maps from our observations on each app, allowing efficient side-by-side comparison. This surfaced what was universally present and what was universally absent: a leisure tool built specifically for caregivers, especially one that reduced the cognitive friction of choosing what to do.

User Personas

Three caregiver archetypes emerged from the interviews:

  • Walter, 50, accountant. Has irregular free hours between work and caregiving. Goals: more physical activity, making use of unpredictable free windows. Frustration: no fixed routine makes planning impossible.
  • Bianca, 62, employee. Arrives home exhausted after a full day plus caregiving for her father. Wants to relax and read, but tends to fall asleep in front of the TV. Fears she doesn't have enough time for mindfulness.
  • Irene, 53. Works mornings; afternoons split between caregiving and brief personal moments. Feels guilt about spending time on herself. Already uses video course apps.
User personas card grid — Walter, Bianca, Irene

Card Sorting

A hybrid card sorting session with 4 users validated the information architecture. Users sorted app features into self-defined categories, and results were analysed in a heat map table.

Card sorting heat map table

Output: Four main navigation pages — Home, Explore, Saved, Profile — each accessible from a persistent bottom navigation bar.

User Journeys

For each persona, a user journey was mapped on Miro, covering: performed actions, thoughts, emotions, touchpoints, and opportunities for improvement at each stage of the app flow.

Branding & Visual Direction

  • Mission: Give caregivers tools to focus on the quality of their free time.
  • Tone of voice: Casual, serious, respectful, matter-of-fact.
  • Name rationale: "FreeMe" was constructed using the theory of sound symbolism (Klink, 2000). The phoneme [i] makes brand names feel milder, friendlier, and lighter.
  • Colour palette: A desaturated green primary (#405D60) was chosen for its associations with nature and psychological balance. Colours were tested for colour-blindness compatibility using Coolors.
  • Typography: Nunito (sans-serif, body), paired with Manrope (headings) via Fontjoy and Fontpair.

Prototyping

Sketches & Wireframes

Each team member independently produced hand-drawn sketches, then translated them into Figma wireframes. References: Pinterest and Mobbin for interaction pattern inspiration.

Side-by-side — paper sketch vs. high-fidelity wireframe of the Home page

Key Feature: "Be Inspired" (Lasciati Ispirare)

To address decision fatigue, the Home page leads with a smart suggestion module: users select a mood category and a time window, and the app pre-filters the Explore page.

"Since I have to make decisions every day for me and others, I like having someone that gives me an idea and doesn't make me think about what to do." — Caregiver.

Gamification

Designed using Yu-kai Chou's Octalysis Framework, targeting Creativity & Feedback (personal course projects) and Development & Accomplishment (badges and progress bars).

Progress page showing level indicators, minute counters, and badges per category

Evaluation

Cognitive Walkthrough

We ran a cognitive walkthrough for each user persona through the wireframes.

Issues found: The "Be Inspired" section lacked onboarding explanation; the timer needed clearer signage; navigation elements needed icon support.

Actions taken: Added brief explanatory copy to "Be Inspired"; made "See all >" links typographically more prominent.

Cycle 2

Prototyping

Design System & Mockups

Built in Figma. Components defined: Colours (Primary #405D60, Secondary #00272B), Type scale (Nunito, Manrope), Icons (Rounded set). Stitch and Visily were used for inspiration. Content for activities was generated using Hyperwrite and ChatGPT. The logo was generated using Kittle.

Evaluation

Think-Aloud with Real Users

Tested with 4 participants and 11 tasks. Evaluated using a 3-level Task Success Rate scale.

66% Success (full task completion, independent)

25% Partial Success (completed with difficulty)

9% Failure (task not completed without guidance)

Task success rate table — 11 tasks × 4 users

Problematic Tasks:

  • Task 1: Users didn't recognise the save icon without a label.
  • Task 9: Confusion between "Resume where you left off" and "Courses started" pages.
  • Task 11: The Activities/Courses toggle at the top was ignored and invisible in context.

Cycle 3

Prototyping

Targeted Fixes

  • Fix 1 (Task 1): Added text label ("Salva") next to the bookmark icon.
  • Fix 2 (Task 9): Merged overlapping pages into a single "In corso" (In Progress) page on the Profile.
Before/After — two separate pages merged into "In corso"
  • Fix 3 (Task 11): Moved the toggle from the top to the bottom (standard CTA area) and increased dimensions.
Split-screen before/after of the Task 11 toggle repositioning

Evaluation

Heuristic Evaluation

Used Nielsen Norman Group's Heuristic Evaluation Workbook to assess the revised prototype against all 10 heuristics.

Heuristic evaluation annotated screens
  • Visibility of system status: No violations.
  • Consistency & standards: Inconsistency noted for Podcasts placement between Explore and Saved.
  • Flexibility & efficiency: Opportunity for onboarding flow to personalise the Home.

Outcomes & Reflection

What worked

  • "Be Inspired" solved a genuine cognitive pain point, validated by users.
  • 3-category structure (Relax, Move, Courses) held up perfectly through all cycles.
  • Gamification (Octalysis) added meaningful motivation without complexity.

What didn't work

  • Icon-only navigation was insufficient; verbal labels were required.
  • Mobile prototype performance forced us to conduct tests on desktop, limiting ecological validity.

Ethical consideration

An emotional profiling feature was designed for "Be Inspired" — the app would ask users their energy level and emotional state before suggesting activities. This was removed during review for ethical reasons related to sensitive data collection from a vulnerable population.

Future opportunities

  • Onboarding flow with initial preference profiling → personalised Home
  • Podcast elevated to a standalone macro-category (currently a sub-category of Relaxation, inconsistent with its placement in Saved)
  • Additional filters: difficulty level, required materials, user rating
  • Settings page and subpages (currently unprototyped)
  • Scroll position reset on previously visited pages
